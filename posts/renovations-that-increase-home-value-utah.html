How to Increase Your Home's Value With Strategic Renovations

Not all renovations are created equal when it comes to resale value. Some projects pay you back almost dollar for dollar. Others feel great to live with but don't move the needle much at sale time. If maximizing your home's value is the goal, here's where to focus your dollars in Utah.

High-ROI Renovations for Utah Homes

1. Basement Finish

In Utah's market, a finished basement can add significant value — often returning 70–75% of the project cost in added home value, while also making your home far more competitive when you list. Buyers in Utah County expect finished basements, and an unfinished one can actually hurt your sale price.

2. Kitchen Updates

You don't have to do a full gut remodel to see returns. Mid-range kitchen updates — new countertops, cabinet refinishing, updated hardware and lighting — consistently return 60–80% of their cost. A full luxury remodel often returns less on a percentage basis, but makes your home much easier to sell.

3. Bathroom Remodel

A mid-range bathroom remodel returns around 60–70% on average. New tile, updated fixtures, and a fresh vanity make a huge impression. Master bathroom updates tend to have the highest impact on buyers.

4. Deck or Outdoor Living Space

Utah homeowners love outdoor living, and a well-built deck or patio consistently attracts buyers. A composite deck typically returns 65–70% of its cost — and it makes your home stand out during showings.

5. Flooring

Old, stained, or worn flooring is one of the fastest ways to lose buyers. New LVP or hardwood throughout the main living areas can return close to 100% of the investment by simply making the home feel move-in ready.

6. Fresh Interior Trim and Paint

One of the highest ROI projects you can do. New baseboards, door casings, and a fresh coat of neutral paint make a home feel new — for a fraction of what structural renovations cost.

Renovations With Lower ROI (But Still Worth Doing for Enjoyment)

  • Luxury master suite additions
  • Swimming pools (high cost, limited buyer appeal in Utah's climate)
  • Over-improving beyond neighborhood comps
  • Sunrooms and specialty additions

These can absolutely be worth doing if you plan to enjoy them for years — just don't expect to recoup the full cost at sale.

The Best Strategy

Focus on projects that make your home feel complete, move-in ready, and competitive with comparable homes in your neighborhood. The goal isn't to have the fanciest house on the block — it's to have a well-maintained, updated home that buyers feel confident about.
