Do I Need a Permit to Finish My Basement in Utah?

It's one of the most common questions we get: "Can we just skip the permit?" We get it — permits mean inspections, fees, and time. But the short answer is: yes, you almost certainly need a permit to finish your basement in Utah, and skipping it can cause serious problems down the road.

When Is a Permit Required?

In virtually every Utah city and county, a building permit is required when finishing a basement if the work includes:

  • Framing new walls
  • Electrical work (outlets, lighting, panel upgrades)
  • Plumbing (adding a bathroom, wet bar, or laundry)
  • HVAC modifications or extensions
  • Adding a bedroom (which also requires an egress window)
  • Installing drywall in new spaces

In other words — almost any basement finish beyond paint and flooring requires a permit.

What Does the Permit Process Look Like?

  1. Submit plans — Your contractor submits drawings of the proposed work to your city's building department
  2. Plan review — The city reviews and approves the plans (typically 1–4 weeks)
  3. Construction begins — Work starts once the permit is approved
  4. Inspections — An inspector visits at key stages (framing, rough electrical, rough plumbing, insulation, final)
  5. Final approval — Once all inspections pass, you receive a certificate of occupancy

What Does a Permit Cost?

Permit fees in Utah vary by city and project value, but for a typical basement finish you can expect to pay $500–$1,500 in permit fees. It's a small fraction of the overall project cost and well worth it.

What Happens If You Skip the Permit?

This is where homeowners get into serious trouble:

  • Selling your home — Unpermitted work must be disclosed to buyers and can kill a sale or significantly reduce your home's value
  • Insurance claims — If a fire or water damage occurs in an unpermitted space, your insurance company may deny the claim
  • Required demolition — Cities can require you to tear out unpermitted work and redo it with proper permits
  • Safety risks — Inspections exist to catch real problems — faulty wiring and improper egress windows can be life-threatening
